BLS vs ALS Quick Reference Guide

Global Medical Response - Agent Training Resource

🚨 When to Escalate to ALS

  • Life-threatening conditions: Chest pain, stroke symptoms, severe breathing difficulty, unresponsive patients
  • Critical vital signs: Abnormal heart rhythms, severe hypotension, respiratory distress
  • Advanced interventions needed: IV medications, cardiac monitoring, advanced airway management
  • High-risk patients: Cardiac history, trauma with unstable vital signs, suspected overdose

BLS Basic Life Support

Non-emergency and stable patient transport

Typical Patients

  • Stable vital signs
  • Routine dialysis transports
  • Scheduled medical appointments
  • Facility-to-facility transfers (stable)
  • Discharge transports going home
  • Non-urgent wheelchair transports

Equipment & Capabilities

  • Basic first aid supplies
  • Oxygen administration
  • Automated External Defibrillator (AED)
  • Spinal immobilization
  • Bleeding control
  • CPR capability
  • Basic airway management

Crew Certification

  • Emergency Medical Technician (EMT-Basic)
  • BLS certification required
  • Cannot administer medications (except oxygen)

Cost

  • Lower cost option
  • Appropriate for most routine transports

ALS Advanced Life Support

Critical care and emergency transport

Typical Patients

  • Unstable vital signs
  • Chest pain / cardiac events
  • Stroke or neurological emergencies
  • Respiratory distress
  • Trauma patients
  • Unconscious or altered mental status
  • Requires IV medications during transport

Equipment & Capabilities

  • Everything BLS provides, PLUS:
  • IV therapy and medication administration
  • Cardiac monitoring (EKG/ECG)
  • Advanced airway management (intubation)
  • Manual defibrillation
  • Emergency medications
  • Ventilator support
  • Blood glucose monitoring

Crew Certification

  • Paramedic (EMT-P) required
  • ACLS certification
  • Advanced medical training
  • Can administer emergency medications

Cost

  • Higher cost due to advanced training & equipment
  • Medically necessary for critical patients

Quick Decision Guide: Patient Condition → Service Level

Patient Condition Recommended Service Reason
Routine dialysis (stable patient) BLS Patient is stable, no advanced care needed
Discharge from hospital to home BLS Patient medically cleared for discharge
Wheelchair transport to appointment BLS Non-emergency, stable patient
Chest pain or cardiac symptoms ALS Requires cardiac monitoring and potential intervention
Difficulty breathing / respiratory distress ALS May need advanced airway management or medications
Stroke symptoms (facial droop, slurred speech) ALS Time-critical, requires advanced assessment
Unconscious or unresponsive ALS Critical condition requiring advanced life support
Patient requires IV medications during transport ALS BLS cannot administer IV medications
Trauma with stable vital signs BLS If vitals are stable and no critical injuries
Trauma with unstable vital signs ALS Requires continuous monitoring and intervention
Post-surgery transfer (stable) BLS If patient is stable and no complications
ICU to ICU transfer ALS or CCT Critical patients require advanced care
Patient on ventilator ALS/CCT Requires advanced respiratory support
Suspected overdose ALS May need medication reversal agents (Narcan, etc.)
Seizure activity ALS May require emergency medications

Key Differences Summary

✅ Choose BLS When:

  • Patient is medically stable
  • Routine scheduled transport
  • No advanced interventions needed
  • Discharge or facility transfer (stable)
  • Non-emergency situation

🚨 Choose ALS When:

  • Patient has critical condition
  • Life-threatening symptoms present
  • IV medications required during transport
  • Cardiac monitoring needed
  • Emergency or time-sensitive transport
